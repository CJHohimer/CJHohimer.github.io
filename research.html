Research Interests

During my graduate work I helped design and develop an autonomous robot for fresh market apple harvesting. The goal was to design an economical harvesting system that would function in the unstructured outdoor modern apple orchard that both limited damage to the apples and tree structure. To that end, a soft robotic end effector was employed to allow compliant interaction with the underlying tree structure and prevent damage the apples due to errors in location and alignment. This system was evaluated in a commercial apple orchard over several harvesting cycles. While the robot was unable to rival that of its human counterparts in picking speed and results showed that the approach could be made economically viable. Additionally, simple pruning and thinning change recommendations were made to greatly improve picking efficiency and reduce produce damage without increasing current labor costs.

As a postdoctoral research fellow in the Biodesign Lab at Harvard I have been designing and testing wearable robotic systems for both injury prevention as well as rehabilitation and recovery. As part of that effort I worked with a Functional Apparel Designer and Occupational Therapist to assess a wearable robotic glove on stroke survivors as a rehabilitation tool. In this effort we looked at existing technology within the lab to create a unique device for stroke recovery. My job was to help design the soft robotic actuators to overcome the challenge unique to stroke spasticity and improve their function over the previous versions for spinal cord injury survivors. This was a coordinated effort with the functional apparel designer to make the device truly wearable and functional.
